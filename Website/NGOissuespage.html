PCA Act

Prevention of Cruelty to Animals Act

The Prevention of Cruelty to Animals Act, 1960, authored by acclaimed dancer and animal lover, Rukmini Devi Arundale, is an Act of the Parliament of India enacted in 1960 to prevent the infliction of unnecessary pain or suffering on animals and to amend the laws relating to the prevention of cruelty to animals. As per the provisions of the law the government of India formed the Animal Welfare Board of India.

It shall come into force on such date as the Central Government may, by notification in the official Gazette, appoint, and different dates may be appointed for different States and for the different provisions comained in this Act.

Definitions : In this Act, unless the context otherwise. requires

(a) "Animal" means any living creature other than a human being,

(b) "Board" means the Board established under Section 4. and as reconstituted from time to time under Section 5A

(c) "Captive Animal" means any animal (not being a domestic animal) which is in capacity or confinement, whether permanent or temporary, or which is subjected to any appliance of contrivance for the purpose of hindering or preventing its escape from captivity or confinement or which is pinioned or which is or appears to be. maimed;

d) "Domestic Animal" means any animal which is tamed or which has been or is being sufficiently tamed to serve some purpose for the use of man or which, although it neither has been nor is intended to be so tamed, is or has become in fact wholly or partly tamed.

Animal Birth Control (ABCs)

A study of the ABC-AR programme and a comparison with the earlier catch-and-kill procedure conclusively shows the success and feasibility of the former in both the control of the street dog population and the incidence of rabies in dogs and humans. - Dr. S. Chinny Krishna

The bond between humans and dogs began somewhere on the Eurasian continent 1214,000 years ago, where the first mutual relationship was born. Approaching early human camps and settlements, food scraps are provided, and wolves quickly become frequently welcomed visitors, warning humans of imminent danger and later hunting wildlife. Helped. This started the domestication of dogs and the creation of a human-animal connection that is second to none. Today, humans overbreed dogs and then abandon them in large numbers, breaking this bond, putting danger to the dogs themselves and putting significant health risks to human society. Too often, authorities facing the problems caused by these dogs turn to mass destruction in the hope of a quick solution, but realize that destruction must continue every year with no end in sight. increase. In addition, by temporarily reducing the number of stray dogs, authorities have improved the survival potential of the remaining dogs and created new opportunities for newly abandoned dogs. It is now recognized that removing extra dogs cannot solve the problem without combining it with other means such as dog registration, contraceptive surgery and public education.

At certain population densities, birth rates and mortality rates are equal, the population is in equilibrium, and population growth is flat. This more realistic explanation of population growth is known as logistic growth. The upper limit for population growth to level off is known as carrying capacity. Each habitat has a specific load-bearing capacity for each species. This particular carrying capacity basically depends on the availability, distribution, and quality of the resources of each species (shelter, food, water). Population densities of higher vertebrates (including dogs) are almost always close to carrying capacity. The decrease in population density due to additional mortality is quickly offset by better breeding and better survival potential. In other words, if the dog is taken away, the life expectancy of the survivor will be longer. This is because dogs have better access to resources and less competition for resources.

Centers


People for Animals, New Delhi

People for Animals, also known as PFA, is India's largest animal welfare organisation with a nationwide network of 26 hospitals, 165 units and 2.5 lakh members. PFA works towards the rescue and rehabilitation of needy animals. It runs shelters, ambulance services, sterilisation programs, treatment camps and disaster rescue missions. PFA actively patrols highways to check against overloading and smuggling of animals for slaughter. Interestingly, PFA is also on the Film and Censor Board of India to check animal abuse in films and has successfully filed cases against films that misuse animals.

Charlie's Animal Rescue Centre, Bengaluru

CARE was started in January 2013 in Bengaluru and is dedicated to housing, rehabilitating, re-homing and giving timely medical aid to stray and abandoned animals. A 10-year-old 3-legged therapy dog became the inspiration behind this trust. They provide 24-hour veterinary care, ambulance services, inpatient facilities and adoption of dogs and cats. They are now building a medical centre which will consist of an OPD, operation theatre, x-ray and scanning facilities for street animals.
You can visit them by clicking here


The Bombay Society For The Prevention Of Cruelty To Animals (BSPCA), Mumbai

A one-of-a-kind in India, BSPCA is a charitable organisation and has been running for over 134 years. Its purpose is to prevent cruelty to animals and provide help and relief to all animals in Mumbai city. The animal hospital works 24 hours a day and treats around 10000 animals every year which includes different species of animals and birds such as emus, geese, vultures, owls, turtles, horses, monkeys, dogs, and many more. They have started new projects such as a cardiac centre, an ICU, a blood bank, an animal birth control centre, an electric crematorium and a shelter for ownerless animals.
You can visit them by clicking here


The Sanjay Gandhi Animal Care Centre (SGACC), New Delhi

The Sanjay Gandhi Animal Care Centre (SGACC) is India's oldest and Delhi's largest all-animal shelter. This all-animal facility was founded in 1980 and happens to be Asia’s largest, with over 3000 animals at any given point. It is equipped with special care units, OPD clinics, modern dental care, operation theatres, and much more. The SGACC mission is to provide sick, wounded and abandoned animals food, shelter, medication, kindness, safety and care for as long as they need it.
You can visit them by clicking here


Visakha Society For The Protection And Care For Animals, Andhra Pradesh

The Visakha Society for the Protection and Care of Animals (VSPCA) in Visakhapatnam, Andhra Pradesh, has been operational since 1996. As mentioned on their website, the VSPCA works to stop the illegal trade in internationally-protected sea turtles, rescues cows and water buffalo too old or injured to be kept by their previous owners, as well as provides permanent happy sanctuary to hundreds of dogs, cats, birds, monkeys, horses, rabbits, tortoises, ducks and other animals who had been suffering severe abuse or neglect.
